Three Directions Experiment

Context: The 2×2 factorial showed the gate is protective (removing it hurts, −0.128 avg) and population training creates larger deltas that are inconsistent. The baseline (gate+self-play, avg +0.264) wins by keeping corrections conservatively small.

Directions Tested

A — Warm-Start Fine-Tune: Load baseline checkpoint, fine-tune with population at lr=1e-5. Preserve conservative structure while adapting to known opponents.
B — Gate-Scheduled Curriculum: Phase 1: gate=1.0, train delta only (full gradient, self-play). Phase 2: unfreeze gate, population training. Separates "what" from "when."
C — Tanh-Bounded Residual: No gate. Hard-bound corrections to [-0.5, +0.5] via tanh. No L2 penalty. Population training.

Key Findings

1. No direction improved over baseline. The original gate+self-play agent (avg +0.256) remains the best. Direction A matched it on average (+0.252) but with much worse robustness. Directions B and C both degraded. This is now the third experiment (2×2 factorial, ablation, three directions) confirming the same result.
2. Larger corrections universally hurt performance. Baseline effective correction: |δ| × gate ≈ 0.096 × 0.40 = 0.038. Dir-A effective: 0.245 × 0.42 = 0.103 → avg -0.003. Dir-B effective: 0.414 × 0.73 = 0.302 → avg -0.148. Dir-C effective: 0.165 × 1.0 = 0.165 → avg -0.220. There is a clear negative correlation between effective correction magnitude and performance.
3. Direction A reveals a specialization-robustness tradeoff. Population fine-tuning at low lr nearly preserved avg performance (+0.252 vs +0.256) but radically shifted the per-opponent profile: +0.506 vs heuristic (+48% over baseline) but -0.005 vs adaptive (-104%). It learned to exploit the weakest opponent at the cost of the strongest. Critically, its cross-opponent delta std dropped (0.0007 vs baseline 0.005) — the corrections became more uniform, not more opponent-specific. Population training taught a generic shift, not opponent adaptation.
4. Direction B achieved genuine opponent-specificity but it didn't help. Cross-opponent delta std 0.058 (12× higher than baseline). Larger corrections vs heuristic (0.48) than vs value_based (0.38). Gate rose to 0.73 (highest ever observed). But overall performance dropped to +0.108. The curriculum successfully separated "learn what corrections look like" from "learn when to apply them" — but the corrections learned in Phase 1 (gate=1, no gating) were too aggressive, and Phase 2 couldn't rein them in. The gate learned to stay open (0.73) because it had never practiced moderation.
5. Direction C confirms that tanh bounding is not a substitute for the gate. Despite clean gradient flow through tanh (no gate attenuation), reasonable delta magnitude (0.165), and lowest final loss (2.87), it produced the worst performance (+0.036). The deltas used ~80% of the ±0.5 bound (range [-0.46, +0.39]) but were not opponent-specific (cross-opp std 0.003). Without a gate to attenuate, every correction is applied at full strength — and most corrections hurt.
6. All delta directions are negative for Direction A. Range: [-0.36, -0.09] across all opponents. The correction network learned to systematically lower value estimates, not to selectively adjust. This is a bias shift, not opponent modeling. It makes the agent more cautious (which helps against heuristic's bluffs) but more passive against adaptive (which punishes passivity).

What Was Learned

The frozen base is already near-optimal for Leduc Hold'em. Across five experimental conditions (2×2 factorial + three directions), every attempt to add meaningful corrections to the base ValueNetwork has either matched or degraded performance. The base was trained to convergence on a game with only 6 cards, 2 betting rounds, and 3 actions. Its value estimates are approximately correct, and any learned correction is more likely to introduce noise than signal. The baseline's success comes not from its correction mechanism but from its correction mechanism being too small to do damage.
The modulated value architecture has reached its ceiling in this game. The architecture V(s, opp) = V_base(s) + gate × Δ(s, opp) assumes there are exploitable patterns in opponent behavior that translate to value adjustments. In Leduc Hold'em: (1) Q and K have near-identical CFR strategies, limiting what beliefs can distinguish; (2) the action space is tiny (fold/call/raise), leaving little room for exploitation; (3) the base is already near-Nash, so deviations for exploitation are risky. The architecture is sound in principle, but the game doesn't have enough exploitable structure to reward it.
Implications for future work. Rather than further refining how corrections are applied (gate shape, bounding, curricula), the more promising direction would be to either: (a) move to a larger game (full Texas Hold'em) where the base is further from Nash and opponent-specific corrections have more room to help, or (b) abandon the correction paradigm entirely and try a different approach to opponent adaptation (e.g., policy-level adaptation, online learning within a session, or MCCFR-based strategies).
